    How to build a study plan that sticks

    1. Be honest about available time. Look at your timetable and commitments, then set a weekly hour budget you can actually keep.
    2. Weight by difficulty and deadlines. Give harder subjects and nearer exams more hours; start from an even split and shift from there.
    3. Block it in your calendar. A plan that is not scheduled is just a wish. Reserve specific slots.
    4. Use focused sessions. 25 minutes on, 5 off keeps concentration high.
    5. Review weekly. Adjust the split as exams approach.

    Proven study techniques

    • Pomodoro: 25 minutes focused, 5-minute break, longer break every four blocks.
    • Active recall: test yourself instead of re-reading — it is far more effective.
    • Spaced repetition: revisit material over increasing intervals to lock it in.
    • Interleaving: mix related topics in a session rather than one subject for hours.
    lightbulb

    Pair active recall with spaced repetition for the biggest gains — quiz yourself today, again in two days, then a week later.

    Key takeaways

    • Set a realistic weekly hour budget, then split it by priority.
    • Schedule the time — an unscheduled plan rarely happens.
    • Study in focused 25-minute blocks, not marathon sessions.
    • Active recall and spaced repetition beat passive re-reading.

    Frequently asked questions

    How do I make a study plan?
    Decide how many hours you can study each week, list your subjects, then divide the hours across them by priority and difficulty. Break each block into focused 25-minute sessions and protect the time in your calendar.
    How many hours a day should I study?
    For most college students, 2 to 4 focused hours a day is realistic and sustainable. Quality of focus matters more than raw hours.
    What is the Pomodoro technique?
    Studying in focused 25-minute blocks separated by 5-minute breaks, with a longer break after four blocks. It keeps concentration high and reduces burnout.
    How do I split study time between subjects?
    Give more time to harder subjects and those with the nearest deadlines. Start with an even split, then shift time toward weaker subjects. The study time splitter above does the even split for you.
